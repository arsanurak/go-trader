# Mission: Quantitative Algorithmic Trading

## Why
You work daily with the go-trader codebase — reading code, observing P&L, applying changes — but the underlying theory is opaque. The goal is to build enough quantitative fluency that you can reason confidently about *why* a strategy does or doesn't work, read backtest output and know what it means, and make informed decisions when the pipeline surfaces a problem.

## Success looks like
- Given a strategy's backtest statistics, you can say whether the edge looks real or overfitted — and why
- You can explain to someone what the regime system is doing and why it exists
- When the circuit breaker fires or Sharpe drops, you know what that implies and what to investigate
- You can read a new strategy's config and have an informed prior about whether it has positive expected value

## Constraints
- Learning happens in conversational sessions, not dedicated study blocks
- You are a software engineer — math notation is fine; abstract academic framing without code anchors is not
- The go-trader codebase is the primary real-world reference throughout

## Out of scope
- Building new strategies from scratch (not yet — understand the existing ones first)
- Options pricing theory, derivatives modelling
- High-frequency trading and microstructure
